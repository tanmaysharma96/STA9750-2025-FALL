1 Overview & Method

1.1 üß≠ Executive Summary

‚úì Consultancy spending is dominated by individual-level engagements, exceeding firm-based contracts in total value
‚úì Individual daily rates exhibit substantial dispersion and right-skewness, making medians more informative than means
‚úì Apparent pricing differences across consultants are driven primarily by contract duration and departmental assignment
‚úì After controlling for duration and department, no statistically or economically meaningful difference persists between local and international consultants
‚úì Contract awards and total spend are moderately concentrated among a small subset of repeat consultants, consistent with reliance on specialized expertise

‚Üí Overall, the consultancy portfolio reflects disciplined pricing, functional specialization, and contract-structure effects, with no evidence of systematic overpricing or geographic bias.

1.2 üö´ Exclusion of Consultancy Travel Costs (methodological refinement)

‚úì Records include both consultancy services and reimbursable travel costs
‚úì Travel-related line items do not represent professional service fees
‚úì All analyses exclude records where COST TYPE indicates travel

‚Üí Reported values reflect consultancy services only, not reimbursable expenses.

# --- robust date parser: handles Excel serials + messy text, never errors ---
parse_po_date <- function(x) {
  x_chr <- as.character(x)

  # Excel serial dates often appear as numbers (or numeric-looking strings)
  excel_num <- suppressWarnings(readr::parse_number(x_chr))
  d_excel   <- suppressWarnings(as.Date(excel_num, origin = "1899-12-30"))

  # Text dates in many formats
  d_text <- suppressWarnings(
    lubridate::parse_date_time(
      x_chr,
      orders = c(
        "Ymd", "Y-m-d", "Y/m/d", "Y.m.d",
        "dmY", "d/m/Y", "d-m-Y", "d.m.Y",
        "mdY", "m/d/Y", "m-d-Y", "m.d.Y"
      ),
      tz = "UTC"
    ) %>% as.Date()
  )

  dplyr::coalesce(d_text, d_excel)
}

# --- helper to standardize "missing code" placeholders ---
fix_missing_code <- function(x) {
  x <- str_squish(as.character(x))
  x <- na_if(x, "#")
  x <- na_if(x, "")
  x <- na_if(x, "Not assigned")
  x
}

1.3 üóÇÔ∏è Data Overview (analytic scope)

‚úì Dataset includes awarded consultancy records with contract value (PO UAC AMOUNT) and duration (ACTUAL NB WKD)
‚úì Daily rates are computed only for individual consultants and only when workdays are valid (> 0)

‚Üí This section clarifies the analytic sample and ensures consistent interpretation of daily rate metrics throughout the report.

1.4 üßπ Data Quality & Cleaning Rules

‚úì Contract value and workday fields are coerced to numeric for calculation consistency
‚úì Records with missing or non-positive workdays are excluded from daily rate calculations
‚úì Extreme values are retained to reflect genuine heterogeneity in consultancy engagements

‚Üí Cleaning rules ensure daily rates are computed reliably and remain defensible for reporting.

3 Diagnostic Analysis (What Drives Observed Rate Differences?)

This section moves beyond descriptive comparisons to examine why daily rates differ.
The analyses below test whether observed variation reflects structural pricing behavior or mechanical effects driven by contract duration, functional assignment, and reporting structure.

3.1 ‚è≥ Duration vs Daily Rate (mechanical driver of dispersion)

‚úì Many extreme daily rates occur when workdays are very small (e.g., 1‚Äì5 days)
‚úì Longer contracts often show lower daily rates, consistent with retainer / bundled scope structures
‚úì This relationship helps separate true pricing differences from arithmetic effects

‚Üí This section explains why the daily-rate distribution is mechanically right-skewed.

Show code

3.1.1 ‚è≥ Duration vs Daily Rate ‚Äî Interpretation

‚úì Extremely high daily rates are disproportionately associated with very short contracts (e.g., 1‚Äì5 workdays)
‚úì Longer-duration contracts tend to exhibit lower daily rates, consistent with bundled or retainer-style scopes
‚úì The negative log‚Äìlog correlation reflects a mechanical arithmetic relationship rather than deliberate price escalation

‚Üí Contract duration is a key driver of dispersion in daily rates and must be controlled for when comparing pricing across groups.
